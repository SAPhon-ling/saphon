Summary

Given the available data, it is not possible to come up with a single analysis of nasal-oral allophony/allomorphy that accounts for all of the observed data of the language. We posit a series of nasal consonants /m, n, ŋ/ with a series of allophones: [ND, D, DN, N]. [DN] allophones only in word-final position immediately following an oral vowel, e.g. /apuan/ [apuadn] 'hitting'. [D] allophones occur in word-initial position before an oral vowel, and in word-medial position between two oral vowels (e.g. [pudaki] 'eel'). [N] allophones occur in word-initial before an nasal vowel, in word-medial position between two nasal vowels, and in word-final position after a nasal vowel. [ND] allophones occur in word-medial position, between a nasal and an oral vowel. Some [D]s which appear at the beginning of a root when the root lacks any prefixes are realized as [ND] when a prefix with a nasal vowel attaches to its left. However, stating that these prefixes have underlying nasal vowels is problematic, and it is much more logically economical to postulate that the alternation is purely morphological.
