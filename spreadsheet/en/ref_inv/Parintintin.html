Citation

Betts, LaVera. 1981. Dicionário parintintín-português português-parintintín. Brasília, DF: Summer Institute of Linguistics. Betts, LaVera. 2012. Kagwahiva Dictionary. Anápolis, GO: Associação Internacional de Linguística SIL:Brasil.

Summary

This summary combines Betts' 1981 Parintintin-Portuguese dictionary and 2012 posthumous Kagwahiva dictionary, given that the first is a Portuguese translation of the latter; page numbers are given for both sources. Betts proposes 7 processes: (1) leftward LO, post-oralization of /m, n/ to [mb, nd] before an oral vowel (1981:8, 2012:4). The author claims these are pre-nasalized allophones but given their occurence before an oral vowel we assume that this is actually a case of post-oralization as opposed to pre-nasalization, as there is no [nasal] feature within an oral vowel that could trigger such a process; (2) leftward LN, nasalization of /j, ɡ, ɡʷ/ to [ɲ, ŋ, ŋʷ] preceding a nasal vowel (1981:8, 2012:4); (3) rightward LN, pre-nasalization of /j, ɡ, ɡʷ/ to [ɲj, ŋɡ, ŋɡʷ] following a nasal vowel (1981:8, 2012:4); (4) bidirectional LN, nasalization of vowels adjacent to a nasal consonant (1981:8); (5) rightward MPP, the suffix -ga '3.SG.MASC' is realized as [-nga] following a root-final nasal vowel (1981:9); (6) rightward XMP, morpheme-initial oral consonants are nasalized followinig a morpheme-final nasal vowel (1981:9). Betts only provides examples for morphemes with initial /p, ɾ, k/, resulting in p → [m], /ɾ/ → [n], and /k/ → [ŋ] however given the lack of a more explicit proposal we assume that undergoers are a set of lexically specified morphemes with initial oral consonant that may include more undergoers than those covered by the author's examples; (7) rightward XMP, pre-nasalization of morpheme-initial oral consonants when following a morpheme-final nasal vowel (1981:9). Like the MPP process above, Betts only provides a restricted list of consonant-initial morphemes that undergo this process, in this case: /p/ → [mb], /β/ → [mb], /ɾ/ → [nd], /ɡ/ → [ŋɡ], /h/ → [nd]. We also consider this list to not be exhaustive and assume that there are more morphemes with different initial oral consonants that undergo this morphophonological alternation, however further investigation is needed. The author notes that "Nasalization occurs regressively within the word." (2012:4), suggesting that there is a leftward process of LDNH that applies to words in Parintintin, but no further discussion is provided.

Notes

No phonemic table is explicitedly provided by the author, rather a pronunciation guide is given depicting Parintintin orthography and phonemes/allophones associated with each grapheme. Given the lack of a clear proposal, some assumptions have been made regarding the phonemic inventory of Parintitin, including: nasal consonants (N) and voiced obstruents (D) are underlying phonemes and contrastive nasal vowels (grouped under the natural class Ṽ) occur in Parintintin. The assumption that voiced obstruents (D) are phonemes in Parintintin is to account for and corroborate Betts' claims of (1) nasalization of /j, ɡ, ɡʷ/ to [ɲ, ŋ, ŋʷ] and (2) pre-nasalization of /j, ɡ, ɡʷ/ to [ɲj, ŋɡ, ŋɡʷ] which would only be possible if we assumed the above facts. In the pronunciation guide the author notes the following: "Os itens léxicos do parintintin são escritos com as letras apresentadas abaixo, que representam a ortografia usada nos livros publicados para os parintintin. Algumas destas letras são fonemas, por si próprias, com os alofones indicados (ex. o g̃ com as formas fonéticas [gy] e [g]). Outras, como m e mb, são alofones de um só fonema, mas são distintas na ortografia." (1981:7). Furthermore the author claims "The following are sets of allophones: (m, mb), (n, nd), (j, nh, nj), (g, ng), (gw, gw, ngw)" (2012:4); given this claim and its format we assume that nasal consonants are underlying and post-oralized/pre-nasalized variants are allophones. The author does not provide a gloss for the alternation of ga~nga however later examples gloss this morpheme as 3.SG.MASC therefore we assume this to be the case.

Citation

Pease, Helen & Betts, LaVera. 1971. Parintintin phonology. In David Bendor-Samuel (ed.), Tupi studies I, 1-14. Norman: Summer Institute of Linguistics of the University of Oklahoma.

Summary

Pease & Betts propose underlying nasal consonants (N) and contrastive nasal vowels (Ṽ) and discuss 6 processes: (1) leftward, post-oralization of /m, n, ɲ, ŋ, ŋʷ/ to [mb, nd, ɲdʒ, ŋɡ, ŋɡʷ] between a nasal and an oral vowel (p. 2, 7); (2) leftward LO/BO, oralization of /ɲ, ŋ, ŋʷ/ to [dʒ, ɡ, ɡʷ] either word-initially preceding an oral vowel or between two oral vowels (p. 2, 7); (3) leftward LO, /m, n/ post-oralize to [mb, nd] at the beginning of a word before an oral vowel (p. 2, 7); (4) leftward LDNH, the right-most nasal consonant or nasal vowel of a morpheme nasalizes the preceding syllable if that syllable contains a nasal consonant. All vowels within that syllable that contains a nasal consonant are nasalized (p. 9). Glottal stop is transparent to this process; (5) rightward MPP, lexically specified suffixes are nasalized when the preceding root contains a nasal vowel. Some suffixes are nasalized but others may only have the first syllable become nasalized (p. 9); (6) leftward LN, oral vowels are nasalized before /m, n, ŋ/ or before a nasal vowel (p. 7-8).

Notes

Pease & Betts mention that "nasalization may be treated on a level higher than that of a syllable" (p. 9), suggesting that nasalization in Parintintin might be triggered due to a suprasegment [nasal] feature but they do not expand on this analysis. Furthermore, they state that "clusters are always entirely oral or nasal" which leads us to believe that LDNH occurs in Parintintin, but no explicit discussion is provided (p. 6).

Citation

Marçoli, Osmar. 2018. Estudo comparativo dos dialetos da língua Kawahib (Tupi-Guarani) Tenharim, Jiahui e Amondawa. - Amondawa

Summary

Marçoli considers three dialects of the Kawahib language: Tenharim, Jiahui, and Amondawa. Amondawa is covered in this section. The author proposes underlying N and contrastive nasal vowels for Amondawa, as well as 3 phonological processes and 1 morphophonological process: (1) leftward BO, /m, ŋ/ eitheer post-oralize to [mb, ŋɡ] or oralize to [b, ɡ], respectively, word-initially before an oral vowel (p.87-90). /n/ only post-oralizes to [nd] word-initially and does not oralize to [d]. The post-oralized and oralized allophones of /m, ŋ/ are in free variation with each other (p.87-90); (2) leftward LO, /N/ post-oralize between two oral vowels; (3) leftward LN, /ʋ, w, j/ nasalize before a nasal V (p.90-91, 94-95, 114) (in Amondawa this is optional for /ʋ/, which is reflected in an additional process block) ; (4) rightward MPP, /p, k/ pre-nasalize to [mb, ŋɡ] when they appear word-initially and following a nasal vowel from the previous morpheme (117, 121).

Notes

Marçoli considers Tenharim, Jiahui, and Amondawa all dialects of the larger Kawahib language (p. 72). Because of this, and given that the author only provides minor differences in harmony processes and allophones for each dialect, the summaries for each dialect are mostly identical. /p/ does not lenite to [w] in Amondawa as it does in Tenharim and Jiahui; palatalization of /t/ to [tʃ] only occurs in Amondawa. The author includes the following regarding nasalized vowels: "Podemos identificar, no estudo dessa língua, dois graus de nasalidade: vogais fortes (fortis) e suaves (lenis), onde as vogais em posição acentuada são plenamente nasais. Vogais fortes ocorrem em sílabas tônicas e também em sílabas átonas, desde que essas estejam entre consoantes nasais. As vogais suaves ocorrem somente em sílabas átonas." (p. 112).
