Citation

Dietrich, Wolf. 1986. El idioma chiriguano: gramática, textos, vocabulario. Madrid: Instituto de Cooperación Iberoamericana. (izoceño)

Summary

Wolf addresses two lects of chiriguano: ava (including ava and ava-chané) and izoceño. For izoceño (covered in this sheet), Wolf proposes underlying postoralized nasals and nasal vowels. The author assumes the following phonological rules: (1) leftward nasal harmony triggered by the leftmost nasal Ṽ with vowels, postoralized nasals, dʲ, and r as undergoers; (2) leftward long distance nasalization triggered by ND with ND as undergoers and slight nasalization of intervening Vs as a result - is blocked by any other consonant; (3) boundary oralization of V at the end of words, except when following w, j, or other vowels; (4) nasal harmony triggered by the nasal suffixes -ɲo, -rã as opposed to a root-internal trigger; (5) local syllabic nasalization of the final V in the root when followed by the nasal suffixes -ramo, -rani, -ma; (6) nasalization of the suffix tei and postposition téi following a root-final nasal vowel.

Notes

Wolf describes a "dissasimilation" rule where if a syllable has an ND, the preceding syllable must not have ND (and therefore must have N), but there is no explicit relation of this to any sort of harmony system. The author also mentions 4 names for lects of chiriguano: ava, ava-chané, izoceño, and tapiete, but groups ava and ava-chané in group "A" (with the occasional specification of ava-chané as Ac) and has izoceño as group "B", and the author states that they did little offical work with the fourth dialect tapiete and just briefly encountered the group (and appears to group it with group B, but doesn't seem to have done enough work with them to have anything from B meaningfully apply to tapiete). The Izoceño dialect is described by Dietrich to be more conservative than Chiriguano-Ava, especially in having less vowel variability than Chiriguano-Ava
