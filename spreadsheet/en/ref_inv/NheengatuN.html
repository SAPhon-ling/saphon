Citation

Moore, Denny, Sidney Facundes & Nádia Pires. 1994. Nheengatu (Língua Geral Amazônica), its history, and the effects of language contact. Museu Paraense Emílio Goeldi, Brazil.

Summary

Moore, Facundes & Pires posit a 3-way contrast between nasal consonants (N), pre-nasalized stops (ND), and voiced oral stops (D) for Nheengatú spoken in the Upper Rio Negro (p. 93, 98). The authors only posit oral vowels (V), claiming that "there are only four vowel phonemes in modern Nheengatu, at least in the dialect studied. Nineteenth century sources often note a fifth vowel, presumably ɨ" (p. 100). Moore et al. propose 2 phonological process: (1) leftward LN, the nasal portion of a pre-nasalized stop nasalizes preceding vowels in a morpheme or across morphemes (p. 98); (2) bidirectional LN, vowels are nasalized either preceding or following /j̃/ (p. 99). There are no transparents or blockers for this process. The authors note that the consonants /m, n/ both occur before or after oral or nasal vowels (p. 99).

Notes

There is occasional free variation between /t/ and /tʃ/ before [i] but not in all cases. "The palatal nasal is analyzed here as (the typically Tupian) ỹ instead of ñ because (1) it is usually a glide phonetically and (2) the vowels on either side are obligatorily nasalized, unlike the cases of nasals m and n" (p. 99, authors' emphasis). Because of this claim has been included as /j̃/ in the natural classes and allophone rows. This description pertains to the Upper Rio Negro variety of Nheengatú, as noted by the authors: "On this basis we present a very brief description of some of the main structural features of the contemporary Nheengatu of the upper Rio Negro, noting obvious resemblances to the structure of its indigenous ancestor or to Portuguese. Unfortunately, no information is yet available on the Nheengatu of other regions and so little can be said about the important question of variation within modern Nheengatu -- which may be considerable" (p. 93). Historically, /ŋ/ existed in Tupinambá and triggered nasalization of the previous vowel but disappeared in Nheengatu (p. 100). <č> is assumed to be /tʃ/ and <š> /ʃ/.

Citation

Borges, Luiz C. 1991. A língua geral amazônica: aspectos de sua fonêmica. Universidade Estadual de Campinas, Campinas, Sao Paulo:Master's thesis.

Summary

Borges proposes a phonemic inventory of Nheengatú spoken in the Rio Negro that includes phonemes from Nheengatú and those borrowed from Portuguese; all phonemes and their allophones have been included below. Borges proposes underlying nasals (N), contrastive nasal vowels (Ṽ) and oral vowels (V), and posits 6 phonological processes: (1) bidirectional LN, where /j/ and /w/ nasalize 'in a nasal environment' (p. 65 - 66). Based on Borges' data, this process is bidirectional and triggered by a nasal vowel that either precedes or follows the glides {w, j}. /j/ either nasalizes to [j̃] or [ɲ] while /w/ nasalizes to [w̃] (p. 65); (2) rightward LN, optional nasalization of stressed oral vowels word-finally before /N/ (p.66). The nasalized vowels are in free variation with their oral counterparts, hence why they optionally nasalize; (3) leftward LN, obligatory nasalization of stressed nasal vowels when preceded by a nasal consonant (p. 83-84); (4) leftward LN, oral vowels in unstressed syllables nasalized when followed by a nasal coda (p. 84); (5) circumdirectional LN, optional nasalization of unstressed vowels when between two nasal consonants (p.85-86); (6) leftward LDNH, vowels and glides /w, j/ are nasalized in the environment of a nasal vowel.

Notes

[f, x, l, v, z, ʒ] are borrowings from Portuguese (p. 69). is probably /ɔ/ based on Borges' description that it is an open mid-back rounded vowel (p. 32); is probably /ə/ given Borges' table of oral vowels (p. 32) that describes a open-mid central unrouded vowel and is considered to be [ɛ]. Some vowel allophones of nasal vowels are in unconditioned free variation with each other. The LDNH process described below appears to be blocked by any consonant other than the glides /w,j/, given the patterns evident in the data, but the author does not explicitly discuss these opacity facts. Therefore, they have been excluded from the LDNH process below.

Citation

Cruz, Aline da. 2011. Fonologia e Gramática do Nheengatú: A língua geral falada pelos povos Baré, Warekena e Baniwa. Netherlands Graduate School of Linguistics.

Summary

da Cruz proposes underlying N and contrastive nasal vowels for Nheengatú as well as 2 phonological processes: (1) rightward LN, voiced stops pre-nasalize after a nasal vowel (p.46-49); (2) leftward LN, /i/ glides to [j] in syllable onsets and then nasalizes to [ɲ] when preceding a nasal vowel (p. 61).

Notes

Voiced and voiceless obstruents are neutralized following a nasal vowel (p. 38); da Cruz's data represents these obstruents with the archiphonemes /P, T, K/ for this process. da Cruz does not consider /kʷ/ or /ʔ/ to be phonemes in Nheengatú, but rather that /kʷ/ is a sequence of . The author mentions a process of oral vowels nasalizing before a nasal consonant (p.59) but does not propose any data for it or expand on its analysis.

Citation

Taylor, Gerald. 1985. Apontamentos sobre o nheengatu falado no rio negro, brasil. Amerindia(10).

Summary

Taylor posits underlying nasal consonants (N), pre-nasalized stops (ND), and contrastive oral (V) and nasal vowels (Ṽ) for Nheengatú, and 1 morphological and 1 phonological process: (1) rightward allomorphy selection, the locative suffix {-pe} nasalizes to {me} following a nasal vowel of the previous morpheme (p. 5, footnote 6); (2) rightward LN, stressed oral vowels nasalize following nasal consonants (p. 5).

Notes

The phonemic inventory and harmony processes in this paper only apply to the Rio Negro variety of Nheengatú. Pre-nasalized stops /mb/ and /nd/ are conserved in the Rio Negro variant of Nheengatú, but are reduced to /m/ and /n/ in the Maué variant (p. 3). For the morphological process below, Taylor notes that "uma vogal nasal pode nasalizar a consoante inicial do sufixo locativo -pe: paranáme (//paraná-pe//; author's emphasis) "no río", atestado em Tapuruquara e no Içana" (p. 5). While the use of "pode" suggests that this process may be optional in the Rio Negro variety of Nheengatú, since no further explicit analysis by the author is provided, Optionality has been included as N/A. Taylor uses <š> to denote a palatal sibilant, which has been interpreted as /ʃ/ (p. 6). There are no page numbers in this manuscript; page numbers are assumed.
