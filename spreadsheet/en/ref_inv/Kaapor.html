Citation

Kakumasu, James. 1964. Urubú phonemics. Summer Institute of Linguistics (SIL). 1-36.

Summary

Kakumasu proposes underlying nasal consonants (N), complex nasal segments (ND), and contrastive nasal vowels (Ṽ) for Urubu Ka'apor. Five phonological processes are posited: (1) leftward LO, optionally post-oralization of /m, n/ to [m~mb, n~nd] before an oral vowel (p.20); (2) rightward LN, nasalization of root-initial voiceless obstruents /p, t, k/ to [mp, nt, ŋk] following a nasal vowel in the preceding prefix (p. 3). There is no data to suggest that /k/ → [ŋk] however given Kakumasu's claim that this process affects voiceless stops, we assume that /k/ can also undergo this process; (3) leftward LDNH, root-final nasal vowels nasalize oral vowels root-internally (p. 25). Kakumasu's data only provides examples pointing towards /ʔ, s/ being transparent; (4) bidirectional LN, optional nasalization of /j/ to [j] ~ [ɲ] preceding or following a nasal vowel (p. 22); (5) bidirectional LN, nasal consonants nasalize adjacent oral vowels. Kakumasu notes the following regarding this process "nasal consonants tend to nasalize vowels contiguous to it" (p. 25) and along with his data, this allows us to conclude that this process is bidirectional.

Notes

Kakumasu's notation has been updated to reflect current IPA: for [ʃ] for [j] for [ɛ, ɛ̃] for [ɔ, ɔ̃] Devoiced vowels are indicated with the following diacritic [i̥]. Kakumasu describes the vowel devoicing rule as "adding frication a velar fricative [x]" in the case of the high vocoids /i, ɨ, u/. This fricative release is similar to Lucy Seki's (2000) proposal for high vowels in Kamayurá. Word-final vowel devoicing is transcribed by the author as a vowel as the following [iI]; we have adapted the following transcription to represent this process [i͡i̥]. Data for /k/ → [ŋk] is not provided by Kakumasu in the pre-nasalization of voiceless obstruents to [NT]s, but given the author's claim that this pre-nasalization occurs "between a nasalized vowel and voiceless stops" (p. 3), we assume that /k/ is part of the natural class of voiceless obstruents that undergo this process. It may also be the case that /kʷ/ undergoes this process of pre-nasalization as well. Kakumasu includes two examples for his LDNH process, /heʔẽ/ → [hẽˡʔẽ] 'tired' and /risã/ → [rĩˡsã] 'cold' (p. 25), which show that /ʔ, s/ are transparent to nasal harmony. It is possible that all voiceless obstruents including /ʔ/ act as transparent, however Kakumasu does not explicitly state this information. His 1968 article does provide data that shows all voiceless obstruents (T) are transparent; this article and its analysis are included separately. He also says "it has been observed that a nasalized vowel occuring syllable finally tends to nasalize vowels preceeding it" implying optionality while data implies obligation - for this reason we have said we are uncertain about the optionality of this process. There is a nasalization process that affects /j/ but the environment is unclear. Some examples suggest a local nasalization process, where a nasal vowel conditions the nasal alternant to its left. But other data appear to contradict this (see p. 20).

Citation

Kakumasu, James. 1968. Urubú phonology. Summer Institute of Linguistics (SIL). 2-24.

Summary

Kakumasu proposes underlying nasal consonants (N) and contrastive nasal vowels (Ṽ) along with 5 phonological processes: (1) leftward LO, optional post-oralization of /m, n/ to [m~mb, n~nd] before an oral vowel (p. 4); (2) leftward LN, optional nasalization of /j/ to [ɲ] before a nasal consonant or nasal vowel (p. 5); (3) bidirectional LN, pre-nasalization of /p, t, k/ to [mp, nd, ŋk] word-medially between two nasal vowels (p. 6); (4) rightward LN, nasalization of oral vowels following /m, n/ (p. 13-14). Kakumasu only provides examples for nasalization of root-final oral vowels. NDs block this process; (5) leftward LDNH, nasalization of oral vowels and /j/ preceding nasal consonants /m, n, ŋ, ŋʷ/ or a nasal vowel. Voiceless obstruents (T) are transparent to this process (p. 14-15).

Notes

Kakumasu's notation has been updated to reflect current IPA: /š/ → /ʃ/ /y/ → /j/ /tš/ → /tʃ/ /ŋ, ŋʷ/ have post-oralized allophones [ŋɡ, ŋɡʷ] however Kakumasu only mentions that their occurence appears in "normal speech" while "in fast speech [ŋ] and [ŋʷ] may be heard" (p. 4-5). No other description regarding their distribution is provided, so we consider this to be a case of free variation. According to the data, [ŋ~ŋɡ] and [ŋʷ~ŋɡʷ] appear in the same environment as [m~mb, n~nd] which makes us consider that this LO process could be applied to all nasals. Pre-nasalization of voiceless obstruents (T) is only briefly addressed in section 1.3. For LDNH Kakumasu notes "Regressive nasalization may extend further than contiguous syllable but usage is variable and it has not been discussed in this paper." (p. 15). This suggests that nasaliation is gradient from the left edge of the word to the trigger. Kakumasu notes that "It is possible to hear a phonetic difference between inherent nasalization and progressive nasalization when they occur syllable finally. For example, when a recorded tape is played backwards, the progressive nasalization is heard beginning orally and changing to a nasalized vowel before coming to the nasal." An inherently nasalized vowel begins nasalized." (p. 14). We understand it as coarticulatory nasalization instead of a spread of the [nasal] feature.

Citation

Caldas, Raimunda B.C. 2009. Uma proposta de dicionário para a língua Ka'apór. Universidade de Brasília: MA Thesis.

Summary

Proposal for Ka'apor dictionary. Caldas proposes underlying nasal consonants (N) and contrastive nasal vowels (Ṽ) along with 4 phonological processes: (1) leftward LO, /m/ post-oralizes to [mb] preceding an oral vowel in a tautosyllabic stressed syllable (p. 36-37); (2) leftward LO, /n/ optionally post-oralizes preceding an oral vowel when it appears in a stressed syllable and syllable onset, resulting in [n~nd] variation (p. 35); (3) leftward LN, /j/ nasalizes to [ɲ] preceding a nasal vowel; (4) leftward LN, oral vowels are optionally nasalized "in a nasal environment" (p.42). Despite Caldas' claim that nasalization occurs "in a nasal environment", her transcriptions suggest that this process is leftward: /pi'nim/ → [pĩ'nim] ~ [pi'nim] 'pintado', /ʔɨm/ → [ʔɨ̃m] ~ [ʔɨm] 'não', /kamɨ/ → [kã'mɨ] 'leite' (p. 42).

Summary

Santos proposes underlying nasal consonants (N) and contrastive nasal vowels (Ṽ) for Urubu Ka'apor along with 2 phonological processes: (1) leftward LO, optional post-oralization of /m, n/ to [mb, nd] preceding an oral vowel in a stressed syllable (p. 42-43); (2) leftward LN, optional nasalization of /j/ to [ɲ] preceding a nasal vowel, resulting in the following variation [j] ~ [ɲ] (p. 51). It is claimed that ŋʷ is only found before oral vowels but ŋʷ does participate in an allophonic alternation, which suggests that it is underlyingly /ŋʷ/, but that it exhibits a peculiar distributional restriction which is not entirely plausible (p. 42)
